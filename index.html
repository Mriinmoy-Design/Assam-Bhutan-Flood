The Crisis

A flood that began in the mountains

Floodwaters in Assam often begin far upstream. Heavy precipitation across Bhutan's mountainous regions rapidly filled river systems, including the Kurichhu River — a key upstream contributor to downstream flooding in the Brahmaputra basin.

Rivers like the Kurichhu do not respect borders. As water levels rise in the highlands, gravity channels the surge southward, accelerating through narrow gorges before entering the wide floodplains of Assam. The Brahmaputra and its tributaries rose above danger marks across district after district.

"Assam's geography makes it uniquely vulnerable — wide floodplains, braided rivers, and low elevation create natural basins where incoming water accumulates rather than disperses."

Chapter 1

The Kurichhu — a river without borders

The Kurichhu originates in eastern Bhutan's highland terrain at elevations exceeding 3,000m. It descends steeply through narrow valleys, crossing the international border near Samdrup Jongkhar before draining into Assam's lowlands.

"When Bhutan's monsoon rains peaked in late June, the Kurichhu carried three times its normal discharge."

The Kurichhu Dam — a 60m hydropower structure — became a focal point as operators managed surging inflows, releasing controlled discharges that added directly to downstream flooding in Chirang and Bongaigaon districts.

Chapter 2

The Brahmaputra basin fills

The Brahmaputra river system drains over 580,000 km² across Tibet, Bhutan, India, and Bangladesh. When its tributaries rise simultaneously, the main channel swells far beyond its banks, spreading laterally across the alluvial floodplain.

Braided channels — the Brahmaputra's defining feature — shift unpredictably during high-discharge events. Water spreads into dozens of inter-channel islands called chars, where some of Assam's most vulnerable communities live.

"The 1,800 km Brahmaputra valley averages 80–90 km in width — a natural receiving basin for every surge from the north."

Chapter 4

The floodplain effect

Assam's wide, low-gradient floodplain sits at 34–130m elevation. With minimal slope to drive drainage, water that enters the plains has nowhere to go quickly. It spreads laterally for tens of kilometres.

Agricultural land — primarily paddy fields at their most vulnerable nursery stage in late June — absorbed the worst. An estimated 1.2 lakh hectares of farmland were affected, wiping out weeks of cultivation.

The char islands of the Brahmaputra — inhabited by over 2 million people — disappeared entirely below the waterline in the most severely affected reaches.

Chapter 5

Human impact across the valley

Beyond numbers, the crisis disrupted daily life across rural and riverine communities. Over 590,000 people were displaced. Relief camps housed thousands in schools and community halls. Food, drinking water, and medicines had to be airlifted to cut-off areas.

District administrations activated emergency protocols, but the speed and scale of inundation outpaced early responses. The National Disaster Response Force (NDRF) deployed teams across 24 districts.

"In Barpeta alone, 78,000 people were displaced in 48 hours — entire communities moved to highway embankments with whatever they could carry."

Ecological Impact

Beyond the human toll

The 2023 floods affected not just people and infrastructure, but one of India's most biodiverse landscapes. Kaziranga National Park — home to the world's largest one-horned rhinoceros population — was inundated, forcing wildlife onto elevated highways.

🦏

Kaziranga inundated

Over 70% of Kaziranga submerged. Rhinos, deer, and elephants moved to NH-715. Animal mortality recorded in flood corridors.

🌊

Char communities

Riverine char islands — home to over 2 million — disappeared below waterline. Evacuation routes were often non-existent.

🌾

Paddy nurseries lost

~1.2 lakh hectares affected. Sali paddy nurseries destroyed at their most critical growth stage, threatening the Kharif harvest.

🏛️

NDRF response

NDRF & SDRF deployed across 24 districts. 700+ relief camps sheltered displaced populations during and after the crisis.

🌲

Bank erosion

High-discharge events accelerate lateral erosion. Land loss along Brahmaputra banks is permanent — displacing families with no land to return to.

🐟

Fisheries disrupted

Beels and floodplain fisheries — lifeline livelihoods for millions — suffered silt disruption, with long-term spawning ground damage.

Conclusion

A recurring geography of risk

The June 2023 crisis was not an anomaly. It was the latest expression of a recurring geographical reality: Assam sits at the base of one of the world's most rainfall-intense mountain systems, with no natural buffer against upstream surges.

To understand Assam's floods is to understand its mountains, its rivers, its borders — and the invisible lines of elevation that decide who loses everything.
